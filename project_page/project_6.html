Carrots and sticks: assessing intervention effectiveness for sustainable mobility systems to reduce GHG emissions

Objective

To combat climate change, governments worldwide implement a variety of policies to encourage sustainable transportation. These interventions range from “carrots” (incentives such as EV subsidies and public transit grants) to “sticks” (disincentives such as carbon taxes and congestion pricing). This project aims to systematically evaluate the real-world effectiveness of these policy levers in reducing greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from the transportation sector.

Methodology

We use quasi-experimental research designs and large-scale, longitudinal datasets from jurisdictions that implemented sustainable mobility policies. Using advanced econometric methods (e.g., difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity), we estimate the causal impact of each policy on outcomes such as vehicle-miles traveled (VMT), mode split, fleet composition, and overall transportation GHG emissions. This enables robust comparisons of behavioral responses and cost-effectiveness across policy packages.

Expected Outcomes

The project will provide evidence-based guidance for policymakers on designing optimal “carrots + sticks” packages to accelerate the transition to sustainable mobility. Outputs will include actionable insights for climate targets (with attention to equity and economic impacts), as well as dissemination through policy briefs, academic publications, and a public-facing interactive tool for exploring scenarios.
